\section{The formation of cores}
Stars form within cold, dense regions of molecular clouds, where local enhancements in the gas density can become gravitationally unstable and initiate collapse. Once a sufficiently overdense region begins to contract, it forms a dense core: a gravitationally bound concentration of gas that represents the earliest observable stage of star formation prior to the formation of a central protostar. Before any internal heating source develops, such a collapsing core is referred to as a prestellar core, characterized by low gas kinetic temperatures ($\sim$10 K), maintained by efficient radiative cooling, and by the absence of an embedded protostellar object.

As the central collapse of the dense core proceeds and the gas density increases, the innermost regions eventually become optically thick to their own radiation. Radiative cooling then becomes inefficient, and the collapse deviates from the initial isothermal, near free-fall phase as thermal pressure begins to rise adiabatically. This transition leads to the formation of the first hydrostatic core (FHSC; \citealt{Larson_1969}), a short-lived, pressure-supported object that marks a key intermediate stage between the prestellar core and the protostellar phase.

When the central temperature reaches $\sim$2000 K, molecular hydrogen begins to dissociate, providing an efficient energy sink that reduces thermal pressure support. This process triggers a second, rapid gravitational collapse, resulting in the formation of a compact, gravitationally bound central protostar. Following this second collapse, the protostar continues to grow by accreting material from its surrounding envelope, while the system enters the protostellar evolutionary stage.

The envelope surrounding the forming protostar is a cold, dusty reservoir of infalling material. It typically extends over thousands to tens of thousands of AU, and in the earliest stages the envelope retains a large fraction of the system's mass. In the early evolutionary stages of young stellar objects (YSOs), commonly referred to as the Class~0 and Class~I phases, the spectral energy distribution is dominated by emission from the envelope \citep[]{Andre_1993, Evans_2009}, and mass accretion from the envelope onto the central protostar and disk remains significant. Within this observational classification scheme, Class~0 and Class~I sources correspond to deeply embedded systems in which a substantial envelope is still present, while more evolved Class~II objects are characterized by a largely dissipated envelope and emission dominated by the protostar. Over time, as accretion weakens and the envelope dissipates, the protostar evolves into a pre-main-sequence star, eventually contracting toward the main sequence where sustained hydrogen fusion begins.

\section{The streamer in observations}
Streamers have been reported across a range of protostellar evolutionary stages, from deeply embedded Class~0 systems (\citealt{Pineda_2020}; \citealt{Valdivia-Mena_2022}; \citealt{Kido_2023}) to more evolved Class~I and Class~II sources (\citealt{Segura_2016}; \citealt{Gahm_2018}; \citealt{Yen_2019}), and in both single and multiple protostellar systems. They are observed in a variety of molecular tracers, including CO isotopologues, H$_2$CO, HC$_3$N, as well as shock-sensitive species such as SO and SiO, revealing elongated, arc-like, or spiral structures extending over hundreds to thousands of astronomical units. 

These structures commonly exhibit systematic velocity gradients, with higher line-of-sight velocities toward smaller radii, and are often misaligned with the disk midplane, indicating that they trace non-axisymmetric infall from the envelope rather than disk-confined spiral features. In several systems, the spatial and velocity structures of streamers have been modeled using kinematic prescriptions that describe ballistic, free-falling gas trajectories with conserved angular momentum in rotating protostellar envelopes, such as the Ulrich--Cassen--Moosman framework or closely related formalisms (\citealt{Aso_2023}; \citealt{Kido_2023}; \citealt{Flores_2023}; \citealt{Kido_2025}). In addition, the detection of shock tracers near streamer termini or at centrifugal barriers suggests the presence of accretion shocks where infalling material interacts with the disk or the disk--envelope interface (e.g., \citealt{Yen_2017, Yen_2019, Pineda_2020, Speedie_2025}).

As a consequence of these observational and kinematic properties, streamers are increasingly recognized as potential channels for transporting material from the envelope to the disk, with possible implications for disk growth, stability, and angular-momentum redistribution (\citealt{Yen_2019}; \citealt{Pineda_2020}; \citealt{Valdivia-Mena_2022}; \citealt{Hsieh_2023}). At the same time, the observational identification of streamers remains challenging (e.g., \citealt{Gahm_2018, Yen_2019, Valdivia-Mena_2022}). They often exhibit only weak density or emission contrasts relative to the surrounding envelope and display a wide diversity of morphologies and orientations, deviating from the classical picture of star formation based on axisymmetric collapse. Projection effects, tracer-dependent sensitivity, and the lack of a unified observational definition further complicate direct comparisons across different sources and datasets.

Among the reported cases, the streamer toward the Class~0 source Per-emb-2 provides one of the clearest examples in which a dedicated kinematic model was applied to interpret streamer gas motions, demonstrating consistency with gravitational infall under a free-fall framework (\citealt{Pineda_2020}). More recent studies have extended such analyses to additional systems, showing that although streamers can act as coherent accretion channels from envelope to disk scales, their inferred mass infall rates are generally smaller than those of the surrounding envelope and contribute only a minor fraction of the total accretion budget (\citealt{Gahm_2018, Yen_2019, Pineda_2020, Valdivia-Mena_2022, Hsieh_2023, Kido_2025}).

\section{Current Limitations}
Streamers are often interpreted as organized channels of infalling material that connect the envelope to the inner disk region. A common way to model such structures is to treat a streamer as a \emph{trajectory} governed by gravity and angular-momentum conservation. The Cassen--Moosman--Ulrich (CMU) model (\citealt{Ulrich_1976}; \citealt{Cassen_1981}) describes the motion of infalling material originating from an initial direction $(\theta_0,\phi_0)$ and evolving along ballistic paths as they fall toward a central mass. Because angular momentum is conserved, the resulting trajectories naturally develop curvature and a systematic line-of-sight velocity gradient, making the CMU model a physically motivated baseline for interpreting streamer-like features.

Another widely used approach is the \emph{streamline} model (\citealt{Mendoza_2009}), in which streamer-like structures are constructed by connecting velocity vectors within a rotating core. This provides a flexible way to generate curved morphologies and to link spatial structure with kinematics. However, an important limitation is that a streamline is fundamentally a mathematical object: it represents the instantaneous direction of the velocity field. As such, it does not correspond to a directly observable emission feature in current observations, which lack time-resolved information and instead trace line emission integrated over a finite volume and velocity range.

\section{Overview of Targets Used in This Study}
To test our model against protostellar systems in which streamer-like structures have been reported, we use four observational datasets shown in Figure~\ref{fig:per2} $\sim$ \ref{fig:SCrA}: Per-emb-2, Per-emb-50, HL~Tau (HL~Tauri), and S~CrA (S~Coronae~Australis). Per-emb-2 and Per-emb-50 were observed with the Northern Extended Millimeter Array (NOEMA), whereas HL~Tau and S~CrA were observed with the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA). For Per-emb-50, HL~Tau, and S~CrA, we extract the position-position-velocity (PPV) information of the streamer directly from the calibrated data cubes, while for Per-emb-2 we work with the moment maps. 

A summary of the observational properties of all datasets is provided in Table~\ref{table:obs_summary}. Additional source properties adopted from the literature, such as the central stellar mass, systemic velocity, and disk orientation parameters, are summarized in Appendix~\ref{tab:observations}. In practice, the disk position angle is used to rotate the observed coordinates into a common model reference frame, as described in Section~\ref{sec:position_angle}, while the disk inclination is reported only for comparison with the model inclination derived from the fitting.

Per-emb-2 was observed in D-configuration on June 22, July 27--28, and August 19, 2018, and in C-configuration on October 9, 10, 17, and 18, 2018 (project S18AG; \citealt{Pineda_2020}). The streamer is traced using the HC$_3$N ($J=10-9$) emission, and we analyze the structure based on the published moment maps. The baselines ranged from 16.4 to 287.8~m. The resulting data cube has a synthesized beam of $3\farcs73\times3\farcs02$.

Per-emb-50 was observed in C-configuration on December 29, 2019, and January 5, 2021, and in D-configuration on August 6, September 7, and 8, 2020 \citep{Valdivia-Mena_2022}. In this work, the streamer is traced using the H$_2$CO ($J=3_{0,3}-2_{0,2}$) emission. The synthesized beam of the cube used in this work is $1\farcs24\times0\farcs72$.

\subsection{Per-emb-2}
Per-emb-2 (IRAS 03292+3039) is a protostellar object in the Perseus molecular cloud at a distance of 300 pc \citep{Zucker_2018}. The source contains a close binary with a separation of around 30 au \citep{Tobin_2018} and shows a clear outflow in CO (2--1) \citep{Stephens_2019}. NOEMA imaging has revealed a streamer structure connecting scales of more than 10,000 au to within a few hundred au of the protostar, interpreted as tracing a globally infalling envelope \citep{Pineda_2020}.

\subsection{Per-emb-50}
Per-emb-50 is a Class~I protostar in the NGC~1333 region at a distance of $\sim$293~pc \citep{Ortiz_2018}. Previous studies have shown that Per-emb-50 is embedded in a collapsing envelope, with large-scale gas kinematics dominated by gravitational infall toward the central protostar \citep{Valdivia-Mena_2022}. It is relatively luminous among sources of similar evolutionary stage and hosts a compact disk with a characteristic radius of $\sim$30~au \citep{Segura_2016}. Molecular outflow activity is observed roughly east--west \citep{Stephens_2019}. The velocity structure along the streamer shows a gradual increase in blueshift toward smaller radii.

\subsection{HL~Tau}
HL~Tau is a Class~I protostellar system located in the Taurus star-forming region at a distance of 147~pc \citep{Galli_2018}. High-resolution ALMA observations have revealed a compact protoplanetary disk exhibiting multiple concentric gaps and rings \citep{Yen_2019}. The HCO$^{+}$\,(3--2) cube used in this work resolves the morphology and velocity pattern of the streamer structure connecting envelope material to the disk. Previous molecular-line observations have shown that HL~Tau remains embedded in a rotating and infalling envelope on scales of several thousand astronomical units \citep{Hayashi_1993,Yen_2019}.

\subsection{S~CrA}
S~CrA is a binary system consisting of two Class~II sources separated by $\sim$200~au \citep{Gupta_2023}, located within the Corona Australis molecular cloud at a distance of 160~pc \citep{GaiaCollaboration_2023}. ALMA observations reveal complex circumbinary gas structures, including filamentary features extending from the envelope toward the central region \citep{Gupta_2023}. The $^{13}$CO (2--1) data cube provides sufficient spatial and spectral resolution to trace these asymmetric accretion flows, making S~CrA an important case for testing dynamical streamer models. Although S~CrA is a more evolved system, spectroscopic and interferometric studies indicate the presence of inflowing gas components and remnant envelope material that can supply asymmetric accretion flows \citep{Gahm_2018,Gupta_2023}.

\section{Our work overview}

The formation process of protoplanetary disks is complex and not yet fully understood, particularly during the early phases when material is transported from the infalling envelope toward the disk. Streamer structures provide a direct observational probe of this mass-transfer process, but their complex morphology and kinematics make them difficult to interpret using existing approaches. Because streamers are observed only in projection, it is often difficult to localize them in three dimensions or to recover their full velocity structure from line-of-sight measurements alone. In addition, it is not always clear whether the observed features trace infall or outflow motions, nor from which regions of the envelope they originate.

In this work, we develop a kinematic modeling framework designed to characterize streamer features in a consistent and physically motivated way, focusing on embedded YSOs during the early Class~0 and Class~I stages, when a substantial infalling envelope is still present. During these phases, the protostar within its parental envelope, and the surrounding environment is both turbulent and gravitationally unstable. Under such conditions, small perturbations within the collapsing envelope can grow nonlinearly, giving rise to asymmetric, filamentary overdensities known as streamers. These streamers represent dense structures embedded within the infalling envelope and, unlike the rotationally supported disk, do not necessarily follow Keplerian motion. By fitting the observed streamer morphology and line-of-sight velocity structure, our model enables us to infer underlying core-scale properties and to quantitatively compare streamer kinematics across different embedded protostellar systems.
