\section{Build up a model}
We construct a kinematic model to reproduce the observed streamer structures by treating them as density-enhanced material undergoing gravitational collapse toward a central protostar. The model describes the three-dimensional kinematic structure of streamer material undergoing radial infall and rotational motion under the influence of gravity and angular momentum conservation. The streamer morphology and kinematics are then obtained by projecting the model trajectories onto the plane of the sky.

\section{Formalism}\label{Sub-sec:Formalism}
In this section, we summarize the formalism of the streamer model, including the characteristic spatial and temporal scales of the collapse, the infall and rotational velocity fields, and the accumulated azimuthal rotation along the streamline. The model is parameterized by the initial polar angle of the streamer ($\theta$), the initial azimuthal angle ($\phi_0$), the inclination of the system ($i$), the characteristic collapse time ($t_{\rm s}$), and the dimensionless ratio of the rotational velocity to the Keplerian rotational velocity ($\omega$).

The characteristic spatial scale of the collapse is defined by the sonic length,
\begin{align}
    r_{\rm s} = c_{\rm s} t_{\rm s},
\end{align}
where $c_{\rm s}$ is the isothermal sound speed. This scale represents the distance traveled by material moving at the sound speed over the collapse timescale and naturally marks the transition between the outer envelope and the inner collapsing region.

The gravitational influence of the central protostar is described by the dimensionless mass parameter,
\begin{align}
    m_0 = \frac{GM_*}{c_{\rm s}^3 t_{\rm s}},
\end{align}
which characterizes the normalization of the self-similar collapse solution and reflects the degree of mass concentration in the infalling envelope \citep[e.g.,][]{Whitworth_1985}. The value of $m_0$ depends on the specific similarity solution adopted. For the Larson--Penston solution, $m_0$ reaches an upper limit of $m_0 \simeq 46.7$ \citep{Larson_1969,Penston_1969}, corresponding to a dynamically collapsing flow, whereas the inside-out collapse of a singular isothermal sphere yields a substantially smaller value of $m_0 \simeq 0.975$ \citep{Shu_1977}, representing a quasi-static initial configuration. In this work, $m_0$ is treated as an effective parameter that sets the overall mass scale of the collapse.

The radial infall velocity is assumed to follow a free-fall-dominated profile with an additional inward offset,
\begin{align}
    v_{\rm inf} = -\sqrt{\frac{2GM_*}{r}} - 3.3\,c_{\rm s},
\end{align}
where the first term represents gravitational free fall toward the central mass and the constant offset term follows the Larson--Penston similarity solution. The latter accounts for the inward motion of the envelope material prior to the formation of a central point mass, capturing the background collapse velocity of the dense core. We adopt a fiducial sound speed of $c_{\rm s} = 200~\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$.

The rotational velocity is parameterized as
\begin{align}
    v_{\rm rot} = \omega\,c_{\rm s}\,\sqrt{m_0}
    \left[
    \left(\frac{r}{r_{\rm s}}\right)^{-1}
    + \left(\frac{r}{r_{\rm s}}\right)^{-1/3}
    \right],
\end{align}
where the bracketed terms introduce a two-component radial scaling. The $(r/r_{\rm s})^{-1}$ term dominates at small radii and describes the rapidly increasing rotational velocity in the inner free-fall-dominated region, while the shallower $(r/r_{\rm s})^{-1/3}$ term becomes relatively more important at larger radii, corresponding to the more slowly evolving outer envelope. The sonic length $r_{\rm s}$ therefore separates the inner collapse regime from the outer envelope regime. The overall amplitude of the rotational velocity is set by $c_{\rm s}\sqrt{m_0}$ and scaled by the dimensionless factor $\omega$.

The accumulated azimuthal rotation along the streamer is given by
\begin{align}
    \phi = \phi_0 + \omega
    \left[
    \sqrt{2}\left(\frac{r}{r_{\rm s}}\right)^{-1/2}
    + \sqrt{m_0}\left(\frac{r}{r_{\rm s}}\right)^{-1/3}
    \right],
\end{align}
which represents the cumulative azimuthal advance of material along the streamline as a function of radius. The $(r/r_{\rm s})^{-1/2}$ term increases rapidly toward small radii, leading to a stronger azimuthal winding in the inner region, whereas the $(r/r_{\rm s})^{-1/3}$ term varies more slowly with radius and contributes primarily at larger scales. The parameter $\phi_0$ sets the initial azimuthal phase, while $\omega$ controls the overall magnitude of the accumulated rotation along the streamer.

\section{Coordinate Transformation}

The three-dimensional geometry of the streamer is described in a spherical coordinate system centered on the protostar. The position of a gas element along the streamer is specified by the radial distance $r$, polar angle $\theta$, and azimuthal angle $\phi$, and is transformed into Cartesian coordinates according to
\begin{align}
    \left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
         x = r\sin\theta \cos\phi, \\
         y = r\sin\theta \sin\phi, \\
         z = r\cos\theta.
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}
This transformation defines the intrinsic three-dimensional structure of the streamer in the model frame.

The radial infall velocity is assumed to be directed along the local radial vector. Its Cartesian components are therefore given by
\begin{align}
    \left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
     v_{x_{\rm inf}} = v_{\rm inf}\sin\theta \cos\phi, \\
     v_{y_{\rm inf}} = v_{\rm inf}\sin\theta \sin\phi, \\
     v_{z_{\rm inf}} = v_{\rm inf}\cos\theta.
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}
These expressions describe the inward motion of material toward the central object.

The rotational motion is assumed to be purely azimuthal. The total velocity field is obtained by combining the infall and rotational components,
\begin{align}
    \left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
         v_x = -v_{\rm rot}\sin\theta \sin\phi + v_{x_{\rm inf}}, \\
         v_y =  v_{\rm rot}\sin\theta \cos\phi + v_{y_{\rm inf}}, \\
         v_z =  v_{z_{\rm inf}}.
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}
This formulation allows the streamer to exhibit both inward collapse and azimuthal motion simultaneously.

\section{Projection onto the Sky and PPV Space}
To enable direct comparison with observations, the modeled positions and velocities are projected into the observational frame by applying an inclination rotation. The inclination angle $i$ is defined such that $i=0$ corresponds to an edge-on configuration, with positive values indicating backward tilting and negative values indicating forward tilting relative to the observer.

The projected spatial coordinates are obtained through a rotation about the $x$-axis,
\begin{align}
    \left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
     x' = x, \\
     y' = y\cos i + z\sin i, \\
     z' = -y\sin i + z\cos i.
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}
Under this convention, the $(x',y')$ coordinates lie on the plane of the sky, while $z'$ corresponds to the line-of-sight direction.

The velocity field is transformed in the same manner to obtain the projected velocity components,
\begin{align}
    \left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
        v_x' = v_x, \\[4pt]
        v_y' = v_y\cos i + v_z\sin i, \\[4pt]
        v_z' = -v_y\sin i + v_z\cos i.
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}
The line-of-sight velocity $v_z'$ is therefore directly comparable to the observed spectral-line velocity in PPV space.

In this framework, $\theta$ specifies the initial polar angle of the streamer, $\phi_0$ defines its initial azimuthal orientation, $i$ sets the viewing geometry, $\omega$ controls the strength of rotational motion relative to Keplerian rotation, and $t_{\rm s}$ determines the characteristic collapse timescale of material along the streamer.

\section{Fitting procedure}\label{sec:fitting_procedure}
The fitting procedure described below is applied to each dataset to constrain the model parameters. The results are compared to the observed streamer morphology and kinematics to assess the validity of the model.

\subsection{Position angle}
Before fitting the kinematic model, all observational centroids are rotated into the model reference frame, which assumes that the projected angular--momentum axis is aligned with the positive declination direction ($+z$). Because each target possesses a different disk position angle (P.A.), the observed coordinates are rotated by the measured P.A. to ensure a consistent geometric convention across sources. Each data point $(x_{\rm obs}, z_{\rm obs})$ is rotated according to
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
x_{\rm mod} \\
z_{\rm mod}
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\cos{\rm PA} & \sin{\rm PA} \\
-\sin{\rm PA} & \cos{\rm PA}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
x_{\rm obs} \\
z_{\rm obs}
\end{pmatrix},
\]
and the rotated coordinates $(x_{\rm mod}, z_{\rm mod})$ are used in all subsequent fitting procedures.

\subsection{Centroid Extraction and Observed Streamer Morphology}

To characterize the observed streamer morphology, we extract a sequence of position--velocity centroids from moment map. Using the protostar position as the origin, we compute pixel offsets $(x_{\rm rel},z_{\rm rel})$ and convert them into polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$. A set of manually selected streamer points defines an interpolated main direction $\theta_{\rm stream}(r)$.

The emission is divided into radial shells. Within each shell, we assign a directional weight
\[
w_\theta = \max\!\left[0,\frac{x_{\rm rel}\cos\theta_0 + z_{\rm rel}\sin\theta_0}{r}\right],
\]
where $\theta_0=\theta_{\rm stream}(r)$. The preliminary centroid is the intensity-weighted mean position,
\[
\langle x\rangle = \frac{\sum(Iw_\theta x_{\rm rel})}{\sum(Iw_\theta)},\qquad
\langle z\rangle = \frac{\sum(Iw_\theta z_{\rm rel})}{\sum(Iw_\theta)}.
\]
The transverse dispersion within the shell provides an estimate of the local lateral width $\sigma_r$.

To obtain the final centroid and velocity, we apply an angular Gaussian weight
\[
w(\theta)=I\exp\!\left[-\tfrac12\left(\frac{\Delta\theta}{\sigma_\theta}\right)^2\right],\qquad \Delta\theta=\pi-\big|\pi-|\theta-\theta_{\rm stream}(r)|\big|,
\]
where
\[
\sigma_\theta=\frac{\sigma_r}{\max(r,\epsilon)},\qquad \epsilon=10^{-3}
\]
so that a fixed physical width corresponds to a smaller angular dispersion at larger radii. The refined position and line-of-sight velocity are computed as weighted averages and converted to physical units using the observational header information.

These centroid points $(x_{\rm cen},z_{\rm cen},v_{\rm cen})$ that traces the streamer direction, providing a noise-suppressed representation for comparison with the kinematic model. The number of centroids extracted for each target is summarized in Table~\ref{table:MCMC_configuration}.

\subsection{Grid search parameter estimation}
We model the streamer kinematics using five parameters ($\theta$, $\phi_0$, $i$, $t_{\rm s}$, $\omega$), corresponding to the initial polar and azimuthal angles of the streamer, the inclination of the system, the characteristic collapse time, and the ratio of rotation velocity. 
For each target, we first perform a coarse five-dimensional grid search to identify reasonable regions of parameter space. The grid covers $\theta \in (0, \pi)$, $\phi \in (-\pi, \pi)$, and $i \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$ with uniform sampling, while the collapse time $t_{\rm s}$ is the characteristic time after the central mass singularity formation, and $\omega$ is sampled linearly between 0 and 1.

The grid search uses the extracted position-velocity centroids to evaluate the model performance. At each grid point, we compute the misfit between the model streamer and the observed centroids using the error metric
\[
E = \sqrt{\left\langle \Delta x^2 + \Delta z^2 + w_v\,\Delta v^2 \right\rangle},
\]
where $\Delta x$ and $\Delta z$ are the projected position differences (in AU), and $\Delta v$ is the line-of-sight velocity residual (in $\rm km\,s^{-1}$). 
The weight
\[
w_v = \left(\frac{\Delta x_{\rm AU}}{\Delta v_{\rm km\,s^{-1}}}\right)^2
\]
is chosen such that spatial and velocity deviations contribute in comparable physical units.

\subsection{Markov Chain Monte Carlo exploration}
After constructing data-driven prior ranges from the grid search, we perform Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) exploration of the five parameters. The MCMC sampling is carried out using the \texttt{emcee} package \citep{Daniel_2013}, which implements an affine-invariant ensemble sampler that efficiently explores correlated and multi-modal parameter distributions.

We use the grid results to construct the priors for the subsequent MCMC exploration. Specifically, we select all grid models with $E \le (1 + f)\,E_{\min}$, where $E_{\min}$ is the minimum grid misfit and $f$ is typically 0.05-0.10 (relaxed, if necessary, to include at least several tens of models). For each parameter, we take the 5th-95th percentile range of these "good" models and pad this interval by $\sim30\%$ to define a broad, uniform prior. This procedure yields data-driven prior bounds that are wide enough to avoid artificially constraining the solution but narrow enough to exclude clearly disfavored regions of parameter space.

Each MCMC run begins by initializing an ensemble of walkers within the prior intervals, distributed in a narrow region around the best-performing grid-search solution. For the centroid-based likelihood, which evaluates the misfit using the extracted position-velocity centroids, this "fast-likelihood" stage enables efficient refinement of the parameter space and helps to identify the dominant modes of the posterior distribution. The adopted numbers of walkers and steps for each target are summarized in Table~\ref{table:MCMC_configuration}.

For the full datacube fitting, we evaluate the agreement between the model and the observed position-position-velocity structure using a shell-based distance metric. Instead of computing distances for every voxel, we construct a shell-distance cube around the model streamer and compare it with the observed cube through intensity-weighted shell distances. This shell-based likelihood allows the MCMC to probe the full three-dimensional structure while remaining computationally tractable. The corresponding MCMC configurations (walkers, steps, and whether the shell-based likelihood is used) are also listed in Table~\ref{table:MCMC_configuration}.

Convergence of the chains is evaluated using the autocorrelation time of each parameter, as well as visual inspection of walker trajectories. An initial burn-in phase is discarded, and the remaining samples are used to compute posterior medians and credible intervals as the final estimates of the five parameters.
