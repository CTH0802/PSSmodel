\section{Methodological considerations}
During the MCMC exploration, we occasionally encounter runs that terminate abnormally or converge unrealistically fast. These cases are characterized by near-zero acceptance fractions, undefined or NaN autocorrelation times, and extremely short execution times, indicating that the ensemble of walkers fails to effectively explore the parameter space. Such behavior most likely arises when walkers become trapped in regions of vanishing posterior probability, which can be caused by overly restrictive priors, numerical instabilities in the likelihood evaluation, or strong parameter correlations that effectively collapse the sampling onto a lower-dimensional subspace. In contrast, successful MCMC runs exhibit longer execution times, acceptance fractions within the expected range, and well-defined autocorrelation times, reflecting efficient sampling of the posterior distribution. We therefore interpret runs with vanishing acceptance rates and undefined convergence diagnostics as non-converged and exclude them from further analysis, retaining only chains that demonstrate stable sampling behavior and statistically meaningful convergence.

We further compare fitting results obtained using moment maps with those derived from the full spectral data cube (e.g., Per-emb-2). While moment maps provide a compact representation of the emission morphology, they inevitably discard information along the velocity axis. In contrast, fitting based on the full data cube retains the complete position--position--velocity structure, enabling a more accurate reconstruction of both the projected streamer geometry and the line-of-sight velocity field.

For parameters that are partially constrained by previous observations, such as the disk position angle and inclination, we adopt a conservative approach by treating them as free parameters in the fitting procedure. This allows the model to independently recover the preferred geometric configuration, which can then be directly compared with literature values, rather than enforcing prior assumptions that could bias the inferred solution.

\section{Implications of inconsistent inclination}
In this work, the inclination derived from the streamer fitting represents the orientation of the large-scale infalling envelope traced by the streamer, whereas literature values of disk inclination are typically obtained from continuum emission or molecular-line observations. When disk inclinations are available, the streamer-derived inclination can therefore be directly compared with the disk orientation after accounting for the geometric conversion between the model and observational reference frames. In these four sources, we find that the inclination inferred from the streamer kinematics differs from the disk inclination reported in the literature. Such discrepancies may indicate a misalignment between the angular momentum of the inner disk and that of the larger-scale envelope, which can naturally arise if the angular momentum of the collapsing core varies with radius. Alternatively, projection effects and uncertainties in tracing different physical components may also contribute to the observed differences. These inclination inconsistencies suggest that streamers may preserve information about the dynamical state of the envelope on larger scales, which is not necessarily aligned with the orientation of the inner disk. As a result, our streamer model provides a complementary probe of the three-dimensional accretion geometry beyond what can be inferred from disk observations alone.

\section{Global motion inferred from the streamer}
The kinematic information inferred from the streamer fitting provides constraints not only on the local velocity structure, but also on the global dynamical state of the infalling envelope. In particular, the characteristic spatial scale of the collapse $r_{\rm s}$ derived from the model represents a scaling radius that sets the relative importance of gravity and angular momentum, and governs the radial dependence of the velocity and density structure of the infalling envelope. For all four sources analyzed in this study, the best-fit values of $r_{\rm s}$ are significantly larger than the radial extent of the observed streamer structures (Table~\ref{table:best_fit_core_properties}). This indicates that the streamers are situated entirely within the free-fall regime of the envelope, where gravity dominates over rotational support. As a result, the overall streamer kinematics are consistent with global infall, despite the presence of curvature and velocity gradients introduced by angular momentum. This result highlights the importance of three-dimensional modeling in interpreting streamer dynamics. While projected position--velocity signatures alone may not uniquely reveal free-fall behavior, the inferred model parameters demonstrate that the observed streamers trace gas undergoing gravitational collapse toward the central star--disk system.

\section{Envelope accretion rates and the role of streamers}
Our model allows an estimate of the mass accretion rate associated with the large-scale envelope collapse feeding the star--disk systems. When inferred from our model fitting, the resulting envelope-to-disk accretion rate is systematically larger than the accretion rates previously attributed to individual streamers alone (see Section~\ref{sec:Mass_Accretion_Rate_and_Rotational_Energy}). This comparison suggests that, while streamers may trace localized channels of infalling material, their contribution to the overall disk mass budget is likely subdominant, and they may not significantly alter the global disk structure.

\section{Model limitations and alternative interpretations}
Finally, in cases where the model fails to reproduce the observed streamer morphology or kinematics, this discrepancy may indicate that the underlying structure arises from physical processes not captured by the current framework, such as alternative formation mechanisms or interactions beyond simple gravitational collapse and rotation. These cases highlight the limitations of the model and motivate further investigation into the diversity of streamer origins.
