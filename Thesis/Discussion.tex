\section{Discussion}

\subsection{MCMC convergence behavior and quality control}

During the MCMC exploration, we occasionally encounter runs that terminate abnormally or converge unrealistically fast. These cases are characterized by near-zero acceptance fractions, undefined or NaN autocorrelation times, and extremely short execution times, indicating that the ensemble of walkers fails to effectively explore the parameter space. Such behavior most likely arises when walkers become trapped in regions of vanishing posterior probability, which can be caused by overly restrictive priors, numerical instabilities in the likelihood evaluation, or strong parameter correlations that effectively collapse the sampling onto a lower-dimensional subspace.

In contrast, successful MCMC runs exhibit longer execution times, acceptance fractions within the expected range, and well-defined autocorrelation times, reflecting efficient sampling of the posterior distribution. We therefore interpret runs with vanishing acceptance rates and undefined convergence diagnostics as non-converged and exclude them from further analysis, retaining only chains that demonstrate stable sampling behavior and statistically meaningful convergence.

\subsection{Envelope accretion rates and the role of streamers}

Our model allows an estimate of the mass accretion rate associated with the large-scale envelope collapse feeding the star--disk systems. When inferred from our model fitting, the resulting envelope-to-disk accretion rate is systematically larger than the accretion rates previously attributed to individual streamers alone (see Section~\ref{sec:Mass_Accretion_Rate_and_Rotational_Energy}). This comparison suggests that, while streamers may trace localized channels of infalling material, their contribution to the overall disk mass budget is likely subdominant, and they may not significantly alter the global disk structure.

\subsection{Treatment of partially constrained geometric parameters}

For parameters that are partially constrained by previous observations, such as the disk position angle and inclination, we adopt a conservative approach by treating them as free parameters in the fitting procedure. This allows the model to independently recover the preferred geometric configuration, which can then be directly compared with literature values, rather than enforcing prior assumptions that could bias the inferred solution.

\subsection{Moment-based versus full PPV fitting}

We further compare fitting results obtained using moment maps with those derived from the full spectral data cube (e.g., Per-emb-2). While moment maps provide a compact representation of the emission morphology, they inevitably discard information along the velocity axis. In contrast, fitting based on the full data cube retains the complete position--position--velocity structure, enabling a more accurate reconstruction of both the projected streamer geometry and the line-of-sight velocity field.

\subsection{Model limitations and alternative interpretations}

Finally, in cases where the model fails to reproduce the observed streamer morphology or kinematics, this discrepancy may indicate that the underlying structure arises from physical processes not captured by the current framework, such as alternative formation mechanisms or interactions beyond simple gravitational collapse and rotation. These cases highlight the limitations of the model and motivate further investigation into the diversity of streamer origins.
