\section{Overview of fitting performance across methods}
We adopt a hierarchical fitting strategy to constrain the five model parameters $(\theta,\phi_0,i,t_{\rm s},\omega)$, combining complementary information from centroid-based and cube-based representations of the data. The initial five-dimensional grid search provides a robust and efficient identification of viable regions in parameter space and typically recovers the correct large-scale streamer orientation and curvature, serving as a reliable starting point for subsequent refinement. We then perform an MCMC exploration using a centroid-based likelihood to tighten the constraints around these viable regions and to quantify parameter uncertainties and correlations. Because the centroid representation compresses the emission at each projected position into a single characteristic velocity, it efficiently captures the global streamer morphology and the dominant line-of-sight velocity gradient. However, this representation does not retain the full velocity distribution along the line of sight. As a result, centroid-based fits do not always provide a clear improvement over the grid-search results, particularly when different parameter combinations produce similar projected trajectories and velocity trends. This limitation motivates the use of fitting approaches that incorporate the full data set, as discussed below.

For datasets with full spectral cubes, we further apply a shell-based likelihood that compares the model against the complete PPV structure. By incorporating the spatially and spectrally resolved information beyond the moment- or centroid-level compression, the cube-based fits generally yield tighter and more stable posterior constraints, reduce the impact of centroid-related information loss, and improve the overall consistency between spatial and line of sight velocity. In several targets, the shell-based likelihood also leads to closer agreement between representative posterior summaries (e.g., peak versus median solutions), indicating that the posterior is better localized when the full PPV information is used.

Across the sample, parameters tied primarily to viewing geometry (e.g., inclination angles $i$) are often relatively well constrained, while the collapse timescale $t_{\rm s}$ and the rotational parameter $\omega$ can show a pronounced trade-off, reflecting that similar projected velocity gradients can be produced by different combinations of infall timescale and rotational support. We assess convergence using standard ensemble-sampler diagnostics and retain only chains with stable sampling behavior and well-defined autocorrelation times. Overall, the agreement among grid, centroid-based MCMC, and cube-based MCMC results supports the robustness of the inferred large-scale kinematic description, while highlighting that incorporating full PPV information provides the strongest and most internally consistent constraints.

\section{Physical Implications}
\subsection{Collapse Scale and Evolutionary Stage}
The best-fit parameters derived from the streamer modeling provide insight into the physical state of collapsing protostellar envelopes and the dynamical role of streamers in the mass assembly process. In the following, we discuss the physical implications of the characteristic scales and parameters constrained by our model. The corresponding numerical values for each target are summarized in Table~\ref{table:best_fit_parameters}.

The characteristic radius $r_{\rm s}$, defined as the product of the isothermal sound speed and the collapse timescale ($r_{\rm s}=c_{\rm s} t_{\rm s}$), can be interpreted as the location of the collapse rarefaction wave front. Within this radius, material has dynamically responded to the formation of the central protostar, while the outer envelope remains closer to its pre-collapse state. The inferred values of $r_{\rm s}$ therefore provide a direct measure of how far the collapse has propagated into the surrounding core. The collapse timescale $t_{\rm s}$ represents the time elapsed since the onset of central star formation, typically of order $\sim10^{5}$--$10^{6}\,\mathrm{yr}$ in our sample (Table~\ref{table:best_fit_parameters}). This places the observed systems in an early protostellar phase, consistent with the presence of extended infalling envelopes and prominent streamer structures. Variations in $t_{\rm s}$ among sources may reflect differences in their evolutionary stage or initial core conditions.

\subsection{Streamer Geometry and System Orientation}
The inferred streamer geometry further indicates that the infalling material does not necessarily originate from the disk midplane. From the best-fit results, the initial polar angle $\theta$ is consistently found to deviate from the disk midplane value ($\theta = 90^\circ$), indicating that the streamers are launched from envelope material above or below the disk plane. This systematic offset suggests that streamer formation is not confined to the disk midplane, but instead traces accretion flows originating from a range of vertical locations within the collapsing envelope, supporting a three-dimensional picture of mass accretion rather than a purely flattened, disk-like inflow.

The inclination angle $i$ derived from the streamer kinematics provides an independent estimate of the system viewing geometry. We note that the inclination parameter in our model does not correspond directly to the conventional disk inclination, which is defined as the angle between the disk rotation axis and the line of sight. Instead, $i$ describes the geometric rotation applied to the model streamer structure to project it onto the plane of the sky.

Under this definition, $i = 0^\circ$ corresponds to a configuration in which the disk plane lies entirely in the plane of the sky (i.e., an edge-on disk), and increasing $|i|$ tilts the system toward a more face-on orientation. Accordingly, when disk inclinations $i_{\rm disk}$ are available from continuum or molecular-line observations, we convert them to our model inclination using $i = -\left(90^\circ - i_{\rm disk}\right)$. This convention is adopted consistently for all targets analyzed in this work.

When disk inclinations are available from continuum or molecular-line observations, the streamer-derived inclination can therefore be directly compared with the disk orientation after accounting for this geometric conversion. In some cases, discrepancies between the two may indicate a misalignment between the angular momentum of the inner disk and that of the larger-scale envelope, which may arise if the core angular momentum varies with radius or if the system has experienced asymmetric accretion during its formation.

\subsection{Mass Accretion Rate and Angular Momentum Transport}
Using the inferred collapse timescale, we estimate the characteristic mass accretion rate through the envelope onto the disk as $\dot{M}_{\rm env} \sim M_*/t_{\rm s}$, where $M_*$ is the adopted central stellar mass for each target (Table~\ref{tab:observations}). This quantity represents the average accretion rate associated with the global collapse of the envelope, rather than the instantaneous accretion rate onto the star. The resulting accretion rates are typically of order $10^{-6}$--$10^{-5}\,M_\odot\,\mathrm{yr^{-1}}$, consistent with expectations for embedded protostars. These values imply that streamers can act as dynamically important channels for delivering mass from large envelope scales down to the disk, potentially contributing significantly to disk growth during early evolutionary stages.

Finally, the dimensionless rotational parameter $\omega$ characterizes the strength of rotational motion relative to free-fall collapse and provides a proxy for the initial rotational energy of the collapsing core material feeding the streamer. Larger values of $\omega$ correspond to stronger azimuthal motion and more pronounced curvature in the streamer, whereas smaller values indicate a collapse dominated by radial infall.
