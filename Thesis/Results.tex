\section{Overview of fitting performance across methods}
We adopt a hierarchical fitting strategy to constrain the five model parameters ($\theta$, $\phi_0$, $i$, $t_{\rm s}$, $\omega$), combining complementary information from point and global method representations of the data. The initial five-dimensional grid search provides a robust and efficient identification of viable regions in parameter space and typically recovers the correct large-scale streamer orientation and curvature, serving as a reliable starting point for subsequent refinement. We then perform an MCMC exploration using a point method likelihood to tighten the constraints around these viable regions and to quantify parameter confidence intervals and correlations. Because the point representation compresses the emission at each projected position into a single characteristic velocity, it efficiently captures the global streamer morphology and the dominant line-of-sight velocity gradient. However, this representation does not retain the full velocity distribution along the line of sight. While the point method likelihood generally refines the grid-search solution and quantifies confidence intervals efficiently, it compresses the emission at each projected location into a single characteristic velocity and therefore cannot capture the full line-of-sight velocity distribution. This limitation motivates the use of fitting approaches that incorporate the full data set, as discussed below.

For datasets with full spectral cubes, we further apply a global method likelihood that compares the model against the complete PPV structure. By incorporating the spatially and spectrally resolved information beyond the moment map or points compression, the global method fits generally yield tighter and more stable posterior constraints, reduce the impact of point method information loss, and improve the overall consistency between spatial and line of sight velocity. Compared with the point method likelihood, the global method likelihood typically produces a more consistent with the additional kinematic information provided by the full PPV cube.

Across the sample, the geometric parameters that primarily set the streamer orientation and viewing geometry (e.g., $\theta$, $\phi_0$, and $i$) are typically well constrained. In contrast, the collapse timescale $t_{\rm s}$ and the rotational parameter $\omega$ often exhibit a pronounced degeneracy, reflecting that similar projected velocity gradients can be produced by different combinations of infall timescale and rotational support. We assess convergence using standard ensemble-sampler diagnostics and retain only chains with stable sampling behavior and well-defined autocorrelation times. Overall, the agreement among grid, point method MCMC, and global method MCMC results supports the robustness of the inferred large-scale kinematic description, while highlighting that incorporating full PPV information provides the strongest and most internally consistent constraints.

\section{Summary of best-fit parameters}
In this section, we summarize the best-fitting results for the four targets, focusing on the fitting approach that provides the most self-consistent representation of the observed streamer morphology and kinematics in each case. Because different method emphasizes different aspects of the data, the best method can vary from source to source.

\subsection{Per-emb-2}
For Per-emb-2, we focus on the results obtained from the MCMC point method analysis, which provides the most consistent representation of the streamer morphology and kinematics for this target. The best-fit models reproduce the projected streamer structure well in both the moment--0 and moment--1 maps (Figs.~\ref{fig:per2_results_mom0} and \ref{fig:per2_results_mom1}), indicating that the large-scale spatial geometry and the global line-of-sight velocity gradient are robustly captured.

Both the posterior peak and median solutions from the MCMC point method analysis produce very similar projected streamer trajectories and velocity patterns. Their close agreement in the moment maps suggests that the inferred geometry is not sensitive to the specific choice of representative posterior parameters for this source.

Because the fitting is performed using moment maps, each projected position contributes only a single characteristic velocity. Consequently, the comparison in the position--velocity ($z$--$v$) diagram (Fig.~\ref{fig:per2_results_z-v}) is not intended to reproduce a full velocity distribution at a given location. Nevertheless, both the peak and median models follow the overall trend of the observed emission in the $z$--$v$ plane, demonstrating consistency with the global kinematic behavior of the streamer.

The posterior distributions shows the one-dimensional histograms and two-dimensional projected distributions illustrate the parameter constraints and correlations. (Fig.~\ref{fig:per2_results_cornerplot}) reveal noticeable degeneracy between $(\phi_0, \omega)$, indicating that multiple combinations of geometric orientation and rotational parameters can reproduce similar projected morphologies and velocity trends when only moment map information is used.

In addition, a pronounced degeneracy is found between the collapse timescale $t_{\rm s}$ and the rotational parameter $\omega$ in the Per-emb-2 results, as different combinations of these two parameters can produce similar projected velocity gradients. Despite these degeneracies, the posterior distributions remain confined to a well-defined region of parameter space. The resulting confidence intervals (shown as $\pm34\%$ ranges in Fig.~\ref{fig:per2_results_cornerplot}) therefore characterize a solution that consistently reproduce the observed streamer morphology and kinematics.

\subsection{S CrA}
For S~CrA, we focus on the posterior peak solutions obtained from two MCMC fitting approaches: MCMC point method and MCMC global method. In both cases, the fitting is performed after masking the central region containing the close binary system, with a mask diameter of approximately 560~au (dashed circle in Fig~\ref{fig:SCrA_results_mom0}). This masking is applied to exclude emission dominated by the binary system and to enable a more direct and reliable comparison between the model and the streamer component.

Both methods provide a comparable reproduction of the projected streamer morphology in the moment--0 map (Fig~\ref{fig:SCrA_results_mom0}), capturing the overall curvature and large-scale geometry of the streamer. This indicates that the spatial structure of the streamer is robustly constrained and can be reproduced using either point method constraints or full PPV information.

In the velocity domain, both MCMC point method and MCMC global method peak solutions recover the main line-of-sight velocity trend seen in the moment--1 map and the $z$--$v$ diagram (Fig~\ref{fig:SCrA_results_mom1}), demonstrating that the dominant kinematic behavior of the streamer is consistently described by the model. 

Nevertheless, a systematic difference is observed in the outer part of the streamer. In the $z$--$v$ diagram (Fig.~\ref{fig:SCrA_results_z-v}), both MCMC peak solutions exhibit a velocity offset of approximately $\sim$0.5~km~s$^{-1}$ at large projected distances, where the model predicts slightly slower line-of-sight velocities compared to the observed emission. This offset is most apparent in the outer streamer region and likely reflects limitations in constraining the kinematics where the emission becomes weaker and more spatially extended.

The posterior distributions derived from two diffetent method show similar parameter constraints and correlations (Fig.~\ref{fig:SCrA_mcmc_grid_cornerplot} and \ref{fig:SCrA_mcmc_shell_cornerplot}). Overall, the consistency between the MCMC point method and MCMC global method peak solutions demonstrates that the main streamer geometry and kinematics of S~CrA are well recovered.

\subsection{HL Tau}

For HL~Tau, we present the results obtained from the posterior median solutions of the two MCMC fitting approaches: MCMC point method and MCMC global method. In this source, the model fitting is performed using only the redshifted emission associated with the streamer, relative to the systemic velocity, in order to isolate the streamer component from the dominant disk emission.

In the moment--0 and moment--1 maps (Figs.~\ref{fig:HLTau_results_mom0} and \ref{fig:HLTau_results_mom1}), both MCMC point method and MCMC global method median solutions show a close agreement with the observed streamer morphology when projected onto the plane of the sky. The projected model streamers closely trace the spatial distribution of the emission, indicating that the large-scale geometry and global velocity gradient of the streamer are well reproduced by both approaches.

The position--velocity ($z$--$v$) diagrams (Fig.~\ref{fig:HLTau_results_z-v}) further illustrate the consistency of the inferred streamer kinematics in the outer regions. While noticeable differences appear in the inner regions associated with the disk, where the two methods predict different velocity profiles, such discrepancies are expected given that the adopted streamer model does not include a prescription for disk kinematics. In contrast, at larger projected distances, both MCMC median solutions successfully follow the observed velocity trend of the streamer, demonstrating that the outer streamer kinematics are robustly constrained.

The posterior distributions (Fig.~\ref{fig:HLTau_mcmc_grid_cornerplot} and \ref{fig:HLTau_mcmc_shell_cornerplot}) show similar parameter constraints for the two methods, indicating that incorporating full PPV information through the MCMC global method approach does not significantly alter the inferred streamer geometry compared to the MCMC point method for this source. Overall, the results suggest that, when restricted to the streamer emission, both approaches provide a consistent and reliable description of the streamer structure and kinematics in HL~Tau.

\section{Physical Implications}
\subsection{Collapse Scale and Evolutionary Stage}
The best-fit parameters derived from the streamer modeling provide insight into the physical state of collapsing protostellar envelopes and the dynamical role of streamers in the mass assembly process. In the following, we discuss the physical implications of the characteristic scales and parameters constrained by our model. The corresponding values for each target are summarized in Table~\ref{table:best_fit_core_properties}.

The characteristic radius $r_{\rm s}$, defined as the product of the isothermal sound speed and the collapse timescale ($r_{\rm s}=c_{\rm s} t_{\rm s}$), can be interpreted as the location of the collapse rarefaction wave front. Within this radius, material has dynamically responded to the formation of the central protostar, while the outer envelope remains closer to its pre-collapse state. The inferred values of $r_{\rm s}$ therefore provide a direct measure of how far the collapse has propagated into the surrounding core. The collapse timescale $t_{\rm s}$ represents the time elapsed since the onset of central star formation, typically of order $\sim10^{5}$--$10^{6}\,\mathrm{yr}$ in our sample (Table~\ref{table:best_fit_core_properties}). This places the observed systems in an early protostellar phase, consistent with the presence of extended infalling envelopes and prominent streamer structures. Variations in $t_{\rm s}$ among sources may reflect differences in their evolutionary stage or initial core conditions.

\subsection{Streamer Geometry and System Orientation}
The inferred streamer geometry further indicates that the infalling material does not necessarily originate from the disk midplane. From the best-fit results, the initial polar angle $\theta$ is consistently found to deviate from the disk midplane value ($\theta = 90^\circ$), indicating that the streamers are form from envelope material above or below the disk plane. This diversity in theta suggest that streamer structures do not necessarily form within the disk midplane, but is instead a core properties that does not correlate with the angular momentum direction. Supporting a three-dimensional picture of mass accretion rather than a purely flattened disk-like inflow.

The inclination angle $i$ derived from the streamer kinematics provides an independent estimate of the system viewing geometry. We note that the inclination parameter in our model does not correspond directly to the conventional disk inclination, which is defined as the angle between the disk rotation axis and the line of sight. Instead, $i$ describes the geometric rotation applied to the model streamer structure to project it onto the plane of the sky.

Under this definition, $i = 0^\circ$ corresponds to a configuration in which the disk plane lies entirely in the plane of the sky (i.e., an edge-on disk), and increasing $|i|$ tilts the system toward a more face-on orientation. Accordingly, when disk inclinations $i_{\rm disk}$ are available from continuum or molecular-line observations (see Table~\ref{tab:observations}), we convert them to our model inclination using $i_{disk}^{'} = -\left(90^\circ - i_{\rm disk}\right)$. This convention is adopted consistently for all targets analyzed in this work (Table~\ref{table:best_fit_parameters}).

\subsection{Underlying core density}
The background core density profile is motivated by self-similar solutions of gravitational collapse \citep{Penston_1969} and can be written as
\begin{align}
    \rho = 8.8 \frac{\alpha}{4\pi G t_{\rm s}^2}
\end{align}
where $t_{\rm s}$ is the collapse timescale.

We adopt a parameterized form for $\alpha$,
\begin{align}
    \alpha = \sqrt{\frac{m_0}{2}}\left(\frac{r}{r_{\rm s}}\right)^{-\frac{3}{2}} + A \left(\frac{r}{r_{\rm s}}\right)^{-2}
\end{align}
where $r_{\rm s}=c_{\rm s}t_{\rm s}$ is the characteristic collapse radius (\ref{eq:rs}), $m_0$ is the dimensionless mass accretion parameter (\ref{eq:m_0}), and $A$ is a dimensionless coefficient of order unity.

\subsection{Mass Accretion Rate and Rotational Energy}\label{sec:Mass_Accretion_Rate_and_Rotational_Energy}

Using the inferred collapse timescale, we estimate the characteristic mass accretion rate through the envelope onto the disk as 
\begin{align}
    \dot{M}_{\rm env} \sim \frac{M_*}{t_{\rm s}} = m_0\frac{c_{\rm s}^3}{G}, 
\end{align}
where $M_*$ is the adopted central mass for each target (Table~\ref{tab:observations}). The mass infall rate onto the central system is characterized by $m_0$, the level of mass concentration of the self-similar collapse solution. Since this is a snapshot fit to the global collapse solution, which could slowly evolve toward solutions with different m0, this quantity represents the instantaneous mass accretion rate. The resulting accretion rates are typically of order $10^{-6}$--$10^{-5}\,M_\odot\,\mathrm{yr^{-1}}$ in our sample (Table~\ref{table:best_fit_parameters}).

Previous observational studies have reported streamer-related infall or accretion rates of order $10^{-7}$--$10^{-6}\,M_\odot\,\mathrm{yr^{-1}}$ \citep[e.g.,][]{Pineda_2020,Valdivia-Mena_2022,Gahm_2018,Gupta_2024}, which are generally lower than the global envelope accretion rates (Table~\ref{table:best_fit_parameters}). This comparison suggests that individual streamers are not likely to represent the main contributors to the overall mass budget of the disk.

Finally, the dimensionless rotational parameter $\omega$ characterizes the strength of rotational motion relative to free-fall collapse and provides a proxy for the initial rotational energy of the collapsing core material feeding the streamer. Larger values of $\omega$ correspond to stronger azimuthal motion and more pronounced curvature in the streamer, whereas smaller values indicate a collapse dominated by radial infall.
